    • How To Grow Barley Crop

    Introduction

    Barley (Hordeum vulgare L.) is popularly called "Jau" in Hindi and is one of the most important types of grain after rice, wheat and maize. In India, this plant is grown in temperate tropical regions as a summer crop and in tropical regions as a winter crop. Barley also plays an important role in industrial consumption.

    Soil and climate conditions

    Climatic conditions –

    Barley can be grown as both summer and winter crops. This crop is mainly grown as a rabi seasonal crop in India. This culture requires a temperature of 12 ° C to 16 ° C when growing and about 30 ° C to 32 ° C when mature. This plant is very sensitive to frost in every growth phase.

    Soil conditions –

    Barley can be grown on a variety of soils including saline, sodium, and lighter soils. However, this culture thrives best on medium-heavy sandy to loamy soils that have a neutral to medium salt reaction. Fertility Acid soils are not suitable for growing barley.

    Cultivation –

    Most farmers practice nursery bed technique. Nursery beds are made involving around 1/twentieth of the complete field region. The paddy seeds are planted in the bed. They are prepared inside 25 days of planting in marsh regions while in higher heights they require around 55 days to become prepared for transplantation.     Irrigation

    2 to 3 irrigation systems are sufficient for optimal performance in barley cultivation. The first watering should be done at the beginning of the crown root, 25-30 days after sowing. The second watering should be done when the panicle emerges, 6575 days after sowing.

    Diseases occurred in crop:

    Name of Disease Caused by Symptoms Management
    Bacterial blight Bacteria Water soaked spots on foliage; shriveling dead leaves; glossy yellow or brown streaks; plant appears stunted, slow plant growth. Use only certified, disease-free seed; treat seeds with a fungicide prior to planting to prevent diseases which allow bacteria to enter easily; practice crop rotation to reduce disease build-up in soil.
    Basal glume rot Bacterium Brown discoloration at base of the glume (bract covering the kernel); dark line where glume attaches to spike; water-soaked spots on leaves; yellow and necrotic spots on leaves. Treat seeds with a fungicide prior to planting to prevent diseases which allow bacteria to enter easily; practice crop rotation to reduce disease build-up in soil; plow crop residue into soil.
    Barley stripe Fungus Small yellow spots on seedling leaves; yellow to tan stripes along leaf blade before heading; red margins on stripes; death of diseased tissue; heads not emerging; plants stunted. Use only certified seed.
    Barley yellow dwarf Virus Stunted growth of plants; yellow green blotches at leaf tip, leaf margin or leaf blade; leaves turning bright yellow, red or purple. Grow resistant or tolerant varieties; avoid planting crop very early or very late when aphid populations are high.
    Common root rot Fungi Brown lesions on leaves nearest soil extending to stem; resembles drought; death of lower leaves; rotting roots. No chemical treatments for this disease; plant crop in late fall to avoid warm soils which favor emergence of disease; do not fertilize crop excessively; use irrigation to reduce water stress.

    Barley Harvest and Yielding

    Harvest

    The barley harvest is normally ready for harvest at the end of March or mid-April. a property of absorbing moisture from the atmosphere, so the harvest should be stored in a dry place. Mechanical harvesting is preferred when growing barley.

    Yielding

    Harvest yield depends on the variety, farm management practices and weather conditions. An average yield of 18-20 quintals / acre is expected.
