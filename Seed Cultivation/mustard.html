    • How To Grow Mustard Crop

    Introduction

    Mustard is a member of the “Cruciferae” family and is commonly used in Indian cooking. India is the world's fourth largest producer of oilseeds, with rapeseed and mustard accounting for approximately 28.6 percent of total oilseed production. It is the world's third most important oilseed after soybeans and palm oil. In India, mustard seed is used as a condiment in the preparation of vegetables and curries.

    Soil and climate conditions

    Climate Condition

    Subtropical climates are ideal for growing mustard. Mustard thrives in dry and cool climates, so it is typically grown as a Rabi season crop. Mustard crops require temperatures ranging from 10°C to 25°C. Mustard crops are grown in areas with annual rainfall ranging from 625 to 1000 mm. Because this crop cannot tolerate frost, it requires a clear sky and frost-free conditions.

    Soil Conditions:

    Mustard can be grown in a wide range of soil types, from light to heavy soil.Mustard cultivation is best suited to medium to deep soils with good drainage. Mustard grows best in soil with a pH range of 6.0 to 7.5. Make sure to conduct a soil test to determine soil properties such as soil type, strength, and nutritional levels. Raya can be grown in a variety of soil types, ranging from loam to heavy soil.

    Cultivation

    To cultivate one acre, approximately 2 to 2.5 kilogrammes of hybrid seeds are required. A farmer can expect a yield of 6 to 8 quintals from one acre of mustard land. The market price for mustard ranges between Rs.3800 and 4200 per quintal, depending on the quality of the product. Mustard crops last 120 to 125 days on average.

    Sowing -

    Mustard is typically planted in the months of September and October. If the mustard crop is pure, seeds should be sown by drilling; if it is a mixed crop, seeds should be sown by broadcasting or drilling. For even spacing, mix the seeds with fine sand. Seeds should be sown at a depth of no more than 6 cm in the soil for best germination. When planting seeds, make sure there is enough moisture in the soil .

    Fertilizer

    Soil testing is required for accurate fertiliser dosage. Apply Nitrogen : Phosphorus in the form of Urea at 55kg/acre and Superphosphate at 50kg/acre to the toria crop in a 25:8 kg/acre ratio. Only use Potash if the soil is deficient in it. Use a N:P:K ratio of 40:12:6 kg/acre for raya and gobhi sarson, with Urea at 90kg, SSP at 75kg, and MOP at 10kg per acre. App for rainfed raya crop

    Irrigation

    Irrigation should be given prior to sowing the seeds. After sowing the seeds, three irrigations should be applied at four-week intervals. A good amount of organic manure should be applied to the soil; this will help to conserve soil moisture.

    Diseases occurred in crop:

    Name of Disease Caused by Symptoms Management
    Alternaria leaf spot Fungi Small dark spots on leaves which turn brown to gray; lesions may be round or angular and may possess a purple-black margin Plant only pathogen-free seed; rotate crops; applications of appropriate fungicides control disease when present
    Clubroot Fungus Slow growing, stunted plants; yellowish leaves which wilt during day and rejuvenate in part at night; swollen, distorted roots; extensive gall formation Once the pathogen is present in the soil it can survive for many years, elimination of the pathogen is economically unfeasible; rotating crops generally does not provide effective control; plant only certified seed and avoid field grown transplants unless produced in a fumigated bed; applying lime to the soil can reduce fungus sporulation.
    Downy mildew Fungus Irregular yellow patches on leaves which turn light brown in color; fluffy gray growth on the undersides of the leaves Remove all crop debris after harvest; rotate with non-brassicas; application of appropriate fungicides may be required if symptoms of disease are present
    Bacterial black rot Bacterium Irregularly shaped dull yellow areas along leaf margins which expand to leaf midrib and create a characterstic "V-shaped" lesion; lesions may coalesce along the leaf margin to give plant a scorched appearance Primary method of controlling black rot is through the use of good sanitation practices; rotate crops to non-cruciferous crops every 2 years; plant resistant varieties
    Flea beetles Insect Small holes or pits in leaves that give the foliage a characteristic “shothole” appearance; young plants and seedlings are particularly susceptible; plant growth may be reduced; if damage is severe the plant may be killed; the pest responsible for the damage is a small (1.5–3.0 mm) dark colored beetle which jumps when disturbed; the beetles are often shiny in appearance In areas where flea beetles are a problem, floating row covers may have to be used prior to the emergence of the beetles to provide a physical barrier to protect young plants; plant seeds early to allow establishment before the beetles become a problem - mature plants are less susceptible to damage; trap crops may provide a measure of control - cruciferous plants are best.

    Mustard Harvest snd Yielding Process

    Harvesting

    Depending on the variety, the crop matures in 110 to 140 days. Harvest crop when pods turn yellow and seeds become hard. Harvest early in the morning to avoid shattering loss. Using a sickle, cut crops close to the ground. Harvested crops were stacked for 7-10 days. After proper drying, complete the threshing operation.

    Post Harvesting

    Mustard plants should be tied into bundles and dried in the sun for 5 to 6 days. Threshing can be accomplished by striking the mustard plants with a stick. Winnowing is the process of separating grain from husk.

    Yielding

    A yield of 400 kg per hectare is to be expected. Yields can reach 1000 kg per hectare with good variety and management practices.
