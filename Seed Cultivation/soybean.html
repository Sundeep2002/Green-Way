    • How To Grow Saybean Crop

    Introduction

    Soybeans are known as the "GOLDEN BEAN" because they belong to the legume crop family and are primarily indigenous to East Asia. “Glycine max” is its botanical name. Soybean is an extremely nutritive and protein-rich food. They are used as oilseeds because they contain approximately 18% oil. Soybeans are grown in similar conditions to corn.

    Soil and climate conditions

    Climate Condition

    Soybean grows well in warm, moist climates. Temperatures ranging from 26 to 32°C appear to be ideal for most soybean varieties. Soybean farming requires soil temperatures of 16°C or higher to support rapid germination and vigorous seedling growth. A lower temperature may cause flowering to be delayed.

    Soil Conditions:

    Soybean cultivation requires well-drained, fertile loamy soils with a pH range of 6.0 to 7.5. Soybean seed germination is inhibited by saline and sodic soils. Water logging harms crops, so good soil drainage is essential during the rainy season.

    Cultivation

    Sowing -

    The best time to plant soybeans is in mid-June. Soybean farming should be done in lines 45 cm – 60 cm apart using a seed driller or behind the plough. The plant-to-plant distance should be kept between 4 and 5 cm. Under ideal moisture conditions, the sowing depth of soybean seed should be no more than 3 cm – 4 cm.

    Fertilizer

    Soybeans can use nitrogen from the atmosphere, but this is insufficient. As a result, the crop receives 10-15% of its total nitrogen requirement. It is sufficient to apply 12.5 kg of nitrogen per acre and 32 kg of phosphorus per acre. Potash is only required if a deficiency is detected.

    Irrigation

    During the Kharif season, the soybean crop generally does not require any irrigation. However, if there is a long drought at the time of pod filling, only one irrigation will be required. During the rainy season, ensure that crop soil has adequate drainage to avoid water logging. Spring crops would necessitate 6 to 7 irrigations.

    Diseases occurred in crop:

    Name of Disease Caused by Symptoms Management
    Bacterial blight Bacterium Water-soaked spots on leaves which enlarge and become necrotic; spots may be surrounded by a zone of yellow discoloration; lesions coalesce and give plant a burned appearance; leaves that die remain attached to plant; circular, sunken, red-brown lesion may be present on pods; pod lesions may ooze during humid conditions Plant only certified seed; plant resistant varieties; treat seeds with an appropriate antibiotic prior to planting to kill off bacteria; spray plants with an appropriate protective copper based fungicide before appearance of symptoms
    Bacterial pustule Bacteria Tiny pale green spots with raised centers on both upper and lower leaf surfaces which develop raised pustules in lesion center; pustules usually form in lesions on lower leaf surface; mottled brown areas may develop on leaves if lesions coalesce; small red-brown spots may develop on pods of some varieties Plant varieties of soybean that are resistant to bacterial pustule; spray plants with an appropriate protective copper based fungicide before appearance of symptoms
    Rhizoctonia stem rot Fungus Damping-off (pre- or post-emergence death) of seedlings caused by red-brown lesions which girdle the stem; lesions or cankers may be present on the stem of older plants and cause the death of the plant mid-season Treat seeds with an appropriate fungicide prior to planting; plant less susceptible varieties of soybean; plant only in well-draining soils
    Sclerotinia stem rot Fungus Upper leaves of plant become wilted and die; leaves turn a gray-green color and dry out; water-soaked lesions on stem nodes which change color from tan to white; lesions may girdle the stem and infection may spread to both side branches and pods; cottony white mycelial growth occurs on all infected plant parts Do not plant soybeans in fields where common bean, sunflowers or other susceptible crops have been grown the previous season; space plants in narrow rows; avoid excessive irrigation after plants are no longer flowering; plant soybean varieties that are less susceptible to the disease
    Armyworms Insect Singular, or closely grouped circular to irregularly shaped holes in foliage; heavy feeding by young larvae leads to skeletonized leaves; shallow, dry wounds on fruit; egg clusters of 50-150 eggs may be present on the leaves; egg clusters are covered in a whitish scale which gives the cluster a cottony or fuzzy appearance; young larvae are pale green to yellow in color while older larvae are generally darker green with a dark and light line running along the side of their body and a pink or yellow underside Organic methods of controlling armyworms include biological control by natural enemies which parasitize the larvae and the application of Bacillus thuringiensis; there are chemicals available for commercial control but many that are available for the home garden do not provide adequate control of the larvae
